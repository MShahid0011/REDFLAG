How It Works

From case file to verdict

No accounts, no setup, no tracking. Open a case, make a call, see exactly what you got right or wrong. Here's the full walkthrough.

01

You're shown a realistic case

Each of the 12 cases renders as a faithful mockup of the medium it's attacking through — an email client header and body, an SMS thread bubble, or a phone call transcript. The sender, subject line, and message content are all shown exactly as a real target would see them.

EMAIL · SMS · PHONE CALL
02

You decide: legitimate, or social engineering?

Read the case the way you would in real life, then make a single call. There's no trick question and no hidden third option — just the same binary judgment you make every time something lands in your inbox or your phone buzzes.

03

Red flags get highlighted, right in the message

If the case was an attack, every red-flag phrase — a spoofed domain, an urgent deadline, a request for secrecy — gets highlighted directly inside the original message text. Each one comes with a short note explaining exactly why it's suspicious, so the lesson sticks to a concrete detail, not an abstract rule.

04

An analyst note ties it together

Below the highlighted red flags, a short explanation names the attack type (phishing, smishing, vishing, BEC fraud, and so on) and summarizes what made the case dangerous, or, for legitimate cases, why it was safe to trust.

05

A full debrief at the end

After all 12 cases, you get a score, a breakdown of accuracy by attack type (email vs. SMS vs. phone calls), and a short set of field notes summarizing the most important warning signs to carry with you afterward.

Cybersecurity Awareness

How Cybersecurity Attacks Are Detected

📧

Phishing Emails

Phishing emails often impersonate trusted organizations to steal passwords or sensitive information. Warning signs include suspicious links, urgent language, spelling mistakes, and unfamiliar sender addresses.

📞

Vishing Calls

Voice phishing (vishing) uses phone calls to trick victims into sharing personal data or financial details. Detection involves verifying the caller's identity and being cautious of pressure tactics and unexpected requests.

📱

Smishing Messages

Smishing attacks arrive through SMS or messaging apps. They usually contain suspicious links, fake delivery notices, account warnings, or offers designed to create urgency and encourage quick action.

🔳

QR Code Scams

Cybercriminals may place malicious QR codes on posters, emails, or advertisements. Before scanning, users should verify the source and inspect the destination URL for legitimacy.

👤

Impersonation Attacks

Attackers often pretend to be managers, banks, government agencies, or technical support staff. Verification through official channels helps identify and prevent these scams.

🚩

Common Red Flags

Unexpected requests, threats, urgency, secrecy, suspicious links, unusual payment demands, and requests for personal information are among the most common indicators of social engineering attacks.

🔗

Malicious Links

Hover over links before clicking. Misspelled domains, unusual URLs, and redirects to unfamiliar websites are common warning signs.

📎

Dangerous Attachments

Unexpected PDF, ZIP, DOCX, or executable files may contain malware. Verify the sender and purpose before opening any attachment.
